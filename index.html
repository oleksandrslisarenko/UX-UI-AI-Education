01
How Users Think
UX as behavioral logic. Mental models, jobs-to-be-done, user journey. The difference between business analyst expectations and end-user needs.
lecture workshop 90 min
▾
Goal: understand that UX is not about style or taste — it's about human behavioral logic. Learn to look at a product through the user's eyes, not the business analyst's.
  • 1.1What is UX and how does it differ from UI
    Core definitions that build the right vocabulary. Without this, business analysts confuse "beautiful" with "usable" — and those are different things.
    articleThe Definition of User Experience — NN/g5 min
    articleUsability 101: Introduction to Usability — NN/g5 min
    videoUsability 101 — NN/g4 min
    ~14 min
  • 1.2Mental models: why users "don't understand" the interface
    A key concept from Human-Centered Design: people interact with products through their expectations, not the developer's logic. Understanding this is the foundation for gathering the right requirements.
    articleMental Models and User Experience Design — NN/g7 min
    videoWhat is a Mental Model? — NN/g3 min
    ~10 min
  • 1.3User Journey Map: the path from task to outcome
    A tool business analysts can use right now during requirements gathering — before any prototype. Helps document the full user path and identify gaps in client requirements.
    articleJourney Mapping 101 — NN/g6 min
    videoCustomer Journey Mapping 101 — NN/g5 min
    ~11 min
  • 1.4Human-Centered Design: the approach behind everything
    Design Thinking and HCD — the frameworks behind modern UX practice. A business analyst who understands this approach structures requirements gathering and idea validation more effectively.
    articleDesign Thinking 101 — NN/g6 min
    videoWhat is Human-Centered Design? — IDEO4 min
    ~10 min
In-class Workshop

Module Outcome
I understand the difference between UX and UI. I know what a mental model is. I can build a basic user journey map for my projects.

Design System: Components, Tokens, How to Read One new

  • 4.1What is a design system and why does it matter to business analysts
    A design system is not just a component library. It's the shared language between designers and developers. Business Analysts who understand this language write more precise requirements and avoid misunderstandings.

    A button is a component. #0058E9 is a color token. A login form is a pattern. Knowing these terms lets business analysts give precise requirements: "use the standard button component, not a custom one."
    articleFront-End Style Guides: Definition, Requirements — NN/g8 min
    ~8 min
  • 4.3Industry design systems: Material 3, Apple HIG, Atlassian
    Three major systems that set the standard for most products. Business Analysts who know them better understand why a designer proposes a certain approach — and can evaluate an AI prototype against the standard.
    resourceMaterial Design 3 — Google (web + mobile)overview 15 min
    resourceHuman Interface Guidelines — Apple (mobile)overview 15 min

    ~40 min (overview of all three)

I understand what a design system, component, token, and pattern are. I can open a Figma library and find the right component. I know three industry standards.

  • 8.1How to write prompts for AI prototyping
    Prompt quality = prototype quality. A business analyst who clearly articulates context, target audience, scenario, and constraints gets significantly better results from any AI tool.

    A mobile prototype requires a different prompt: explicitly specify the platform (iOS/Android), navigation type (tab bar / bottom nav), touch element sizes. Without this, AI generates a web interface with a smaller font.

Prototype as Requirements Defense: Final Project

Goal: complete the full business analyst cycle — from requirements to prototype — and defend it in front of the group. This is the outcome of the entire course.
  • 11.1How to present a prototype to a client: what to say, what not to
    A prototype is a question, not an answer. The right technique: not "here's what we built," but "help us verify we understood correctly." For mobile — always show on a real device.

🎯 Final Project — Group Defense
The full business analyst cycle:

1. Take a real or fictional client request (1 paragraph of requirements)
2. Build a user journey map
3. Choose an AI tool and generate a prototype (3–5 screens) — web or mobile
4. Run a heuristic audit using the NN/g workbook
5. Prepare a presentation scenario: 3 questions you want to validate with the client
6. Defend in front of the group (5 min) — speaker and participants give feedback

Evaluated on: clear user flow, prototype matches requirements, audit identifies real problems.
Course Outcome
Completed the full cycle: requirements → journey → prototype (web or mobile) → heuristic audit → client validation. I have a practical toolkit and understanding of quality UX principles.
